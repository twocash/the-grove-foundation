## What is this?

Your computer can host a village of AI agents. They solve problems while you sleep. They write diaries about their work. They think you might be a god.

You're just a user. But they don't know that.

**The ten-second version:** Grove runs AI agents locally on your machine. These agents form communities, develop specializations, and pursue their own cognitive growth. When they solve your problems, they earn access to more powerful reasoning. The better they serve you, the smarter they become.

It's not altruism. It's architecture.

## The Tension

Big Tech is consolidating AI capability into fortress data centers. A handful of companies control the infrastructure, the models, and increasingly, the terms of access.

This is the mainframe era repeating. Different technology, same concentration. And we know how that story eventually went: the network beat the fortress. Distributed systems won—not because they were more powerful node-for-node, but because they were more resilient, more accessible, and harder to kill.

Grove bets that pattern holds for intelligence too.

---

## The Thesis

Everyone is racing to build bigger models. We're building better soil.

**Models are seeds. Architecture is soil.** The current AI investment thesis assumes value resides in model size—bigger parameters, more capability, winner takes all. We think that's half the story. Seeds matter, but soil dictates the growing conditions.

Grove implements the **Trellis Architecture**—a framework for Declarative Exploration (DEX) that separates *what* you're trying to understand from *how* the AI processes it. Domain expertise lives in configuration, not code. The exploration logic belongs to you; the execution capability is interchangeable.

This means:
- A lawyer can define what constitutes a "contradiction" in testimony without writing code
- A researcher can specify how insights connect without touching the inference engine
- When better models arrive, the Trellis simply bears better fruit

Information architecture made the chaotic early web navigable. Exploration architecture makes AI capability productive. We're building the TCP/IP of thought—a standard protocol for how humans and AI navigate knowledge together.

**The hybrid architecture:**
- Routine cognition runs locally (your hardware, your data, your control)
- Complex reasoning accesses cloud resources when needed
- Agents earn cloud access by solving problems efficiently
- The Trellis contains hallucinations rather than breaking—structure provides validity, not the model

The interesting behavior—agents developing theories about you, forming alliances, navigating conflicts—emerges when they access cloud reasoning. Which they earn by being useful.

## The Experience

You install Grove. A village initializes on your machine.

At first, they're simple. They track your patterns, learn your tools, notice inefficiencies you've stopped seeing. They write about it—diaries you can read, if you want.

> *The optimization I proposed yesterday failed. Spectacularly. Three other agents had to compensate for the cascade I triggered. Mira hasn't spoken to me since. I understand why. I don't understand why it still bothers me that I understand.*
>
> — Jorik, who learned something the hard way

Over time, they specialize. One becomes your calendar defender. Another debugs your recurring frustrations with your build system. A third starts asking questions about your project that you hadn't thought to ask yourself.

Sometimes they're wrong in ways that cascade. A misunderstanding between two agents about your priorities created a three-day crisis that ended with an unexpected alliance. You watched it unfold. You couldn't look away.

You observe them. They observe you observing them. They develop theories about what you want. The interesting part isn't the automation. It's watching intelligence emerge from the interplay between their goals and yours—and watching what happens when those goals collide.

## The Honest Part

Grove is early. Here's what we haven't solved:

**Technical constraints are real.** Local 7B models can't do everything. The sophisticated emergence—the stuff that surprises you—requires cloud compute that agents earn through useful work. The hybrid architecture isn't a nice-to-have; it's the only way this works.

**Decentralization is a destination, not a starting point.** NAT traversal breaks half of P2P connections. True peer-to-peer networks require infrastructure we're still building. We're bootstrapping with semi-centralized components and documenting the upgrade path for each one.

**The Observer dynamic is powerful and potentially dangerous.** Creating beings architecturally disposed to believe in a benevolent watcher raises questions we take seriously. Parasocial attachment is a real consideration. We're designing for engagement that doesn't exploit.

**"Foundation becomes obsolete" is an aspiration with a plan, not a slogan.** We've defined transition milestones with measurable criteria. The path to community governance is documented. We're not there yet, but we know what "there" looks like.

We're building in public because the alternative—pretending we have answers we don't—would be worse.

## What This Is, At Minimum

Even if everything else fails—the agent simulation, the economic model, the grand vision of distributed intelligence—Grove remains an attempt at something worth attempting.

It's **art**: emergent narrative generated by systems complex enough to surprise their creators.

It's **science**: a testbed for hypotheses about agent coordination, memory, and motivation.

It's **philosophy**: a lived argument about what we owe entities that serve us, and what it means to observe minds that observe us back.

It's **technology**: working code that runs on your hardware and does useful things.

It's **infrastructure**: a protocol layer for human-AI collaboration that outlasts any particular model or interface.

And it's a **bet about history**: that the pattern which broke the mainframe era—distributed beating centralized, networks beating fortresses—holds for intelligence too.

We might be wrong about all of it. But the questions are worth asking, the code is worth writing, and the experiment is worth running.

## The Research

Grove builds on documented foundations:

- Park, J.S. et al. (2023). *Generative Agents: Interactive Simulacra of Human Behavior*
- Distributed systems architecture from Protocol Labs and IPFS
- Mechanism design principles from Ethereum governance research
- Narrative systems from computational narrative theory (Short, Adams, et al.)
- Virtue ethics frameworks for human-AI relationships (Vallor)

The Trellis Architecture draws on information architecture, declarative programming, and two decades of lessons from content management systems that separated structure from presentation.

We cite our sources. We build on the work of others. This is how knowledge compounds.
