The dominant model in current distributed inference networks charges per inference-second or per-token, creating a perpetual rental relationship where users never accumulate equity in the computational infrastructure they rely upon.

This analysis examines what we term the "Ratchet Effect" ‚Äî a systematic dynamic where AI infrastructure costs increase unidirectionally, creating path dependencies that lock organizations into cloud-first strategies regardless of long-term cost efficiency.

The ratchet operates through three primary mechanisms: capability escalation (where newer models require proportionally more compute), data gravity (where accumulated context makes migration prohibitively expensive), and workflow integration (where AI capabilities become embedded in processes faster than alternatives can be evaluated).

Our research suggests that the 21-month doubling time for AI inference costs‚Äîcombined with the 7-month capability refresh cycle‚Äîcreates a window where local ownership becomes economically viable for organizations processing more than approximately 2M tokens per month sustained.

The Grove architecture addresses this through a fundamentally different ownership model: rather than renting access to centralized infrastructure, participants acquire fractional ownership in distributed compute capacity that appreciates with network growth.
